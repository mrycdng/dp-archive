This website acts as both foundation and companion to Marie You’s BFA Graphic Design Degree Project, BEYOND THE SPECTACLE. The texts it houses serve as the ideological platform atop which the DP’s explorative designs rest. Through a multitude of ways, they question and examine the dominating visual culture as well as the “spectacles” it creates.

In response to the DP’s goal to develop a more authentic personal practice, the archive utilises a hypertext structure rich with annotations and external/internal links which make transparent the artist’s lines of inquiry and subsequent personal reflections.

The Future is an Empty Room

But the wind blew through me like I was a hologram. If you say I am a mystic, then fine: I’m a mystic.The trees are not trees, anyway.

Jacob Geller
2026.01.25

[00:08] There’s a moment in the very first episode The Twilight Zone that I keep thinking about. In many ways, that first episode establishes the formula for the entire following series. It  features a main character who is, unbeknownst to him, already embroiled in a highly unusual situation. It stars a single actor, who  gives a big, showy, performance. There’s  a twist at the end that recontextualizes what the whole story was about.

But in other ways, it stands apart from the series. There is no Rod Serling introduction to this episode, no familiar twinkly theme song, no eyeball floating through space. There is, in fact,  no supernatural element to the whole episode. And twenty minutes into the show, this happens:

[00:58] A man sprints down a staircase, seemingly towards the camera, but collides with a mirror.

This… This might be the most memorable moment of the entire series to me. Yes I remember Maple Street and Eye of the Beholder, “room for one more” and “that’s not fair.” But it’s these three seconds, this man desperately trying to pass through the looking glass and being violently rejected, that most viscerally stick in my brain. The strange fracturing of a fantasy.

The entirety of the episode thus far has been this man wandering through an empty town. The restaurants, police stations, and movie theaters are barren. There are hints of life — lit cigarettes in ashtrays, functioning projectors, an everpresent feeling of being watched — but no people, and no explanation of the desolation. At one point, the man remembers he was in the air force and thinks, maybe there was a bomb — maybe it killed everyone. Maybe it killed him. And then he runs into the mirror and he stumbles outside and he begs someone, begs anyone, for help. And then we learn the true shape of his world.

[02:17] The man, Sergeant Mike Ferris, is not in an abandoned town, nor is he dead. For the entire time we’ve watched him, and for hundreds of hours prior, he has been in a single room — an isolation booth, where his entire delusion has been observed by a cohort of uniformed men. This has all been a test, a General explains to a group of reporters. And it was successful: the time spent in the isolation booth, hundreds of hours alone, is what will be required for a trip to the moon. Ferris, despite his mania, proved the ability of humans to last in a room alone. “This is the hardest aspect of endurance,” a medical officer explains.

“You see, Ferris, we can feed stomachs with concentrates. We can supply microfilm for recreation, reading, even movies of a sort. We can pump oxygen in, waste material out.  But there's one thing we can't simulate. That's a pretty basic need -- man's hunger for  companionship, the barrier of loneliness.”

“This is what’s holding us back,” he says. The next frontier, we understand, will be gated not by our strength or beauty or intelligence. The future will be held in an empty room.

[03:48] In the opening of the 2019 video game, Death Stranding... A lot of things happen. We would be here all day if I went through the timefall, the BTs, the bridge babies, the beach. The first forty minutes of the game are a cavalcade of ideas and imagery, all set where most of the game takes place—wide open, lonely highlands, grass and rolling hills and dark cliffs. But after this opening onslaught, Sam, the protagonist, wakes up in effectively a cell. He’s handcuffed to the bed in a windowless room. His new friend, Deadman, tells him that there’s a job only Sam can do: deliver a package of medicine to his mother. Sam’s mother, the president of the United States. His mother, who is being kept in a location referred to as “the isolation ward”. Deadman explains all this, and then he... Glitches. His hologram shimmers. He’s not actually there, see. The room is empty.

[04:53] But you’re allowed to exit your cell and walk back into open air, provided you agree to the mission. The isolation ward where you have  to deliver the medicine isn’t far away. It’s actually very close, like maybe three blocks. Because the walk is so brief, it’s easy to forget how bizarre this environment is, where virtually all of the rest of the game takes place in nature or individual, closed-off bunkers, the walk through “Central” resembles—well, that first episode of the Twilight Zone, actually. It’s an empty main street. Like Ferris in the Twilight Zone, Sam can even holler while he walks. Touch a button on the controller and he’ll yell, “Hey!” or “I’m Sam!” or “Anyone there?” Usually, you’d yell this in the middle of nowhere. Sometimes, you might actually get a response from a nearby player-built structure. But it feels more apt here, among the empty streets. And it’s unnerving to hear nothing but your own echo.

[05:54] That’s not even what I want to talk about, though. After your jaunt through the empty streets, you’ll arrive at the isolation ward, a building that doesn’t seem to live up to its name. What about where I just was, huh? Handcuffed to the bed in a windowless room? This by contrast is big and populated, there are hallways and people and is that- Is that the Oval Office?!

Yes, it is. Sam’s mother, Bridget,  is the president, and though she’s literally on her deathbed, that bed still lies within the sun-dappled curves of America’s most famous room. The life support cables hanging from the ceiling present a potent metaphor: is this the state of America? Its previous symbolism corrupted by the unignorable presence of death? Bridget and Sam argue. She wants to rebuild the country, reconnect. Sam says there’s nothing to rebuild—“Bridget, you’re the president of jack shit”—and Bridget, then, lunges out of bed. Her life support tubes snap, black goo pours out. Sam recoils—forget the medical horror, forget the attack by a near-dead woman—this is the first time he’s been touched, by anyone, in the entire game. She begs him again to help. She dies. And as she flatlines, it seems the room itself does too. Half the population of the office reveal themselves to be holograms as they blink out of existence. The furniture disappears from the walls. The sun disappears from the windows, the windows themselves shudder into a gray void. There was no oval office, no last gasp of American ascendancy. The metaphor  was much more potent than some simple life support cables. This is the true image of the country: three people and a corpse in an empty room.

[07:51] This is a fitting introduction to the future proposed by Death Stranding, a future where “connection” is both  dangerous to attempt and difficult to argue for. Everything has been reconfigured for isolation: immediately after Bridget dies, Sam is tasked with hauling his mother’s body to the crematorium. The massive concrete structure is, predictably, empty. Sam pushes a button and watches her incineration, alone.

Personal Commentary


First of all, I’d like to thank my friend and roommate Maggie for bringing this to my attention. I don’t really play video games at all, so this was a video essay I would’ve definitely missed out on.


While not directly related to graphic design in any aspect at all, Geller, through video game analysis, nonetheless posits what is essentially the ideas shared by other writers in this archive: that reality has been subsumed by representations of that reality—becoming a lush room that ultimately rings hollow. More significantly, he discusses the ramifications of such shallowness: an ever-increasing magnitude of human loneliness as we retreat into illusion. I’m glad to have discovered this, because Geller brings to the table what conversations confined to a design perspective cannot.

THE TWILIGHT ZONE (1959–Present)

An anthology TV series created by Rod Sterling in which characters find themselves dealing with often disturbing or unusual events, an experience described as entering "The Twilight Zone". The episodes are in various genres, including science fiction, fantasy, absurdism, dystopian fiction, suspense, horror, supernatural drama, black comedy, and psychological thriller, frequently concluding with a macabre or unexpected twist, and usually with a moral. Connection

Guy Debord’s seminal work The Society of the Spectacle discusses the phenomenon of image-based commodity fetishism (spectacles) extensively.


E.g. Ch. 2 “Commodity as Spectacle”, Thesis 36:


This is the principle of commodity fetishism, the domination of society by “intangible as well as tangible things,” which reaches its absolute fulfillment in the spectacle, where the tangible world is replaced by a selection of images which exist above it, and which simultaneously impose themselves as the tangible par excellence.

DEATH STRANDING (2019)

An action adventure game developed by Kojima Productions and published by Sony Interactive Entertainment. The game is set in the United States following a cataclysmic event which caused destructive creatures to begin roaming the Earth. The player controls Sam Porter Bridges (Norman Reedus), a courier tasked with delivering supplies to isolated colonies and reconnecting them via a wireless communications network. [Wikipedia]
